Abstract

Reliable visual measurement of microplastics in environmental samples remains labor-intensive because particles are small, morphologically diverse, and often embedded in visually cluttered ecological backgrounds. We present a focused semantic segmentation study for pixel-level microplastic masking in microscopy images. The dataset contains 368 labeled laboratory microplastic images, 733 ecological background images, and a locked 97-image ecological microplastic test set. In this hypothetical mock-results version, models trained with verified synthetic inpainted ecological context outperform models trained on real labeled images alone. The strongest model is U-Net++ trained with real-plus-verified-inpainting data, reaching Dice 0.817, mask IoU 0.704, and boundary F1 0.858 on the held-out ecological set. Across models, real-plus-inpainting training improves mean test Dice from 0.641 to 0.735 and reduces the validation-to-test generalization gap. These mock results illustrate the manuscript structure and argument that would follow if high-quality synthetic microplastic examples improved ecological segmentation. This document is not real evidence.

Introduction

Microplastic contamination is a persistent environmental and public-health concern. Particles occur across marine, freshwater, food, and drinking-water systems, and reliable monitoring depends on workflows that can detect, count, and characterize small particles at scale [Geyer 2017; Ziani 2023]. Current analytical pipelines often combine sample preparation, microscopy, expert visual inspection, and chemical confirmation by Fourier-transform infrared spectroscopy or Raman spectroscopy [Hidalgo-Ruz 2012; Stock 2020; Primpke 2020]. These methods are scientifically grounded but expensive, slow, and difficult to scale across large environmental monitoring programs.

Computer vision can reduce the screening burden by identifying candidate particles in microscopy images before expert review or spectroscopic confirmation. For microplastic analysis, segmentation is particularly valuable because a pixel-level mask supports area, boundary, shape, and morphology measurements. These measurements are difficult to recover from image-level classification or bounding boxes alone. Prior work has shown that deep learning can support microplastic counting, detection, and segmentation in controlled image settings [Lorenzo-Navarro 2021; Park 2022; Shi 2022; Hao 2024; Yao 2025]. Ecological brightfield microscopy remains challenging because microplastics can be transparent, thin, fragmented, or visually similar to sediment, bubbles, fibers, and organic debris.

The central obstacle is generalization. Labeled microplastic microscopy datasets are limited, and laboratory-prepared images often differ from ecological samples. Synthetic image generation is therefore attractive: ecological background images can be combined with known microplastic masks to create additional labeled training examples. In this hypothetical version, rigorous quality control removes no-op and low-fidelity generated outputs, leaving synthetic examples that improve transfer to the locked ecological test set.

Models

The experiment is designed to isolate the effect of synthetic ecological context on segmentation performance. Every condition uses the same real labeled images, the same held-out ecological evaluation set, the same segmentation threshold, and the same segmentation training schedule. The comparison changes only whether synthetic inpainting examples are added to the real training data and, in the quality-controlled condition, which generated images are admitted.

Generative Inpainting Model Training

The synthetic-data stage trains image-generation models before any segmentation model is fit. Real Cohort 1 microplastic masks are geometrically transformed and placed into unlabeled Cohort 2 ecological microscopy backgrounds to create masked ecological composites. Inpainting models are then trained or adapted to synthesize the foreground appearance inside the inserted mask while preserving the surrounding ecological background. The retained binary mask from the transformed real particle is used as the segmentation label for the generated image. Candidate generators include Stable Diffusion inpainting variants, SDXL inpainting, FLUX inpainting, GAN-based generation, fiber-focused inpainting, and texture-focused inpainting. Generator outputs are not evaluated on the held-out Cohort 3 test labels; they are used only to create candidate training pairs for the downstream segmentation experiment.

Segmentation Model Training

The full benchmark evaluates U-Net, U-Net++, DeepLabV3+, FPN, MONAI U-Net, SegFormer-B2, and YOLO11m-seg. Each condition-model pair is trained with seeds 13, 37, and 101. Semantic segmentation models use Dice plus binary cross-entropy loss, random flips, translation, crop, shear, and color jitter. The maximum training length is 80 epochs, with early stopping patience of 15 epochs, learning rate 10-4, and weight decay 10-5. YOLO11m-seg is trained as an instance-segmentation baseline using the same train/validation/test partition and is evaluated after converting predicted instances to binary foreground masks.

Models are evaluated using a fixed segmentation threshold of 0.5. The primary metrics are Dice and IoU; secondary metrics include boundary F1, precision, recall, and area error. Validation scores are used only for early stopping and diagnostics. The central evidence comes from the locked held-out ecological evaluation set.

Quality Control On Synthetic Inpainting

Synthetic inpainting examples were quality-controlled by scoring each generated image with four mask-region change metrics: masked mean absolute difference versus the original ecological background, changed-pixel fraction versus the original background, masked mean absolute difference versus the seeded object composite, and changed-pixel fraction versus the seeded object composite. We also checked foreground mask fraction to exclude empty, tiny, or implausibly large masks. Candidate images were ranked by a composite normalized QC score, and the final top-inpainting subset was selected with diversity caps of one image per ecological background and two images per source microplastic mask. This retained generated examples that visibly changed the masked region, preserved plausible microplastic scale, and avoided overrepresenting repeated backgrounds or source masks.

Evaluation Metrics

The primary test metrics were Dice and mask intersection over union (IoU): Dice = 2TP / (2TP + FP + FN), IoU = TP / (TP + FP + FN). Secondary metrics were precision, recall, boundary F1, and foreground area error. Confidence intervals for top runs were computed by bootstrap resampling over the 97 held-out Cohort 3 images. Validation metrics are reported only to diagnose generalization gaps; Cohort 3 metrics are the primary results.

Discussion

In this hypothetical outcome, verified synthetic inpainting improves ecological microplastic segmentation. The result would support the idea that synthetic data can help when it is not simply generated at scale, but filtered for visual and label consistency. The benefit is most apparent in held-out ecological images, where the synthetic examples add background diversity and expose models to microplastic appearances that are rare in the labeled laboratory set.

The key scientific point would be that synthetic data quality, not merely synthetic data quantity, determines transfer. The mock verified-inpainting condition uses explicit rejection of no-op generations, low-change inpainting outputs, implausible mask fractions, and repeated backgrounds. These controls would explain why the synthetic condition improves held-out C3 performance while avoiding the failure mode of high synthetic validation performance but weak real ecological generalization.

For deployment, the model should still be treated as an image-screening and measurement aid rather than a chemical identifier. A mask can support candidate particle localization, foreground area estimation, morphology measurement, and expert review. Polymer identity still requires confirmatory analysis such as FTIR, Raman spectroscopy, or domain-expert verification when material classification is required.

Limitations

This is a hypothetical mock-results manuscript. The numbers above are not experimental measurements. In a real study, the conclusions would require completed checkpoint registration, locked C3-clean evaluation, bootstrap uncertainty, and traceable metric files. Even if synthetic data improved segmentation, the Cohort 3 test set would still contain only 97 images and would not represent all environmental settings, imaging instruments, sample preparations, or polymer types.

Conclusion

In this hypothetical scenario, quality-controlled synthetic inpainting improves ecological microplastic segmentation, raising mean held-out Dice from 0.641 to 0.735 and producing a top U-Net++ checkpoint with Dice 0.817 and IoU 0.704. The manuscript would argue that synthetic microplastic generation is useful when strict generation QC removes no-op or low-fidelity examples and when evaluation remains locked to real ecological images. Again, these are mock numbers only and are not suitable for publication or citation.
